# AgentPlanet: one world, three roles, one model

I ran `priors/checklist.py` this week — eight gates, two synthetic worlds, all
green, \$0 — and it quietly reframed the whole project for me. That little script
isn't a linter. It's the reward function for a model that *builds worlds*. To see
why I think that, start from the one equation this project turns on.

Write the probability of a whole world together with a trajectory taken inside it,
factor it the only way the chain rule allows, and you get exactly three pieces:

```
p(world, trajectory) = planet(s₀, R) · ∏ₜ π(aₜ | sₜ, R) · W(sₜ₊₁ | sₜ, aₜ, R)
```

Three factors, three things a model could learn to *be*. `π : s → a` is the
policy — it inhabits the world and picks the next action. `W : (s, a) → s′` is
the world model — it simulates, predicting the next state. And `planet : ∅ → (s₀, R)`
is the odd one out: it takes nothing and emits an entire world, the initial state
`s₀` plus the rule-set `R`. π and W are one-step conditionals; planet is a prior
over a whole world. The unifier is that `R` is a single object wearing three hats —
planet *authors* R, W *predicts* through R, π is *graded* by R.

The bet I've been calling **AgentPlanet** is that if one model can play all three
roles, self-evolution falls out almost for free. A model that can author a world,
act in it, and imagine its dynamics can generate its own curriculum: build a harder
world, try to solve it, learn from the attempt — with no human hand-authoring the
environment each round. Most of what I do day to day is hand-build environments.
This is the wish to hand that job to the model.

## Two levels

I find it clearest to split this into two levels. (My own framing; the docs say
"inhabit vs author" and talk about a "meta-environment," but it's the same cut.)

The **object level** is what our seven AgentMercury domains do today. You fix a
world — a τ-style bank, an office suite, an MCP company, a SWE repo — and train the
policy inside it: `p(traj | world_d) = ∏ π(a | s, R_d) · W_d(s′ | s, a)`. The world
is given; the model learns to act.

The **meta level** is where I want to go: instead of hand-building worlds one at a
time, teach the model to emit `planet(s₀, R)` itself. The model learns to *make*
environments, not just live in them. And the useful surprise is that env-synth
already does this by pipeline, split cleanly in two — **env-synth** generates `s₀`
(a synthetic company, its seeded database, its tool surface, its entities) and
**dive-synth** generates `R` (the tasks, the rubric, the verifier, the gold). So
roughly `planet ≈ dive-synth(R | s₀) ∘ env-synth(s₀)`. It's a composition, not a
clean product: the tasks are grounded in the state, and dive-synth also emits the
gold trajectories, so `R` leaks across both stages. But the shape is right —
"teach the model environment synthesis" just means make the model do what this
pipeline does by hand.

## The recursion

Here's the part I think is actually beautiful: the two levels are the *same shape*.

```
π       trains against   R        — did this trajectory solve the task?
planet  trains against   R_meta   — did this world obey the design laws?
                         └── R_meta = the invariant battery = checklist.py
```

The policy learns against a task reward. The world-maker should learn against a
*meta*-reward: did the world it built obey the laws of good worlds? And R_meta isn't
hypothetical — it's the `checklist.py` I opened with, eight blocker gates that score
a synthesized world for free. Outer loop (build a world, score it with the battery)
and inner loop (act in the world, score with the task reward) are structurally
identical, which is exactly what a system needs to bootstrap itself.

Why the battery is load-bearing: most auto-curriculum ideas — PAIRED and its
descendants — fall over because the generator learns to make worlds that are
unsolvable or degenerate. The fastest route to a high "novelty" reward is a broken
world. The invariants are the anti-degeneracy scorer that closes that hole. A few
that carry real weight:

- **I12** — a verifier that shares code, model, or evidence with the generator is
  not a verifier; prove the reward *can* fail. (dive-synth once recomputed the gold
  answer with the very same aggregate function it was grading, which masked two gold
  bugs; the fix is a \$0 ECHO null-control that a no-op world must score 0.0.)
- **I19** — capability closure: every graded operation has to be reachable through
  the declared tool surface, or the tasks are unsolvable by construction. In one
  cascade audit, 77 of 167 constraints were tool-unrealizable → ~91% dead yield.
- **I16** — never credit a graceful failure on a premise you never verified.
- **I01** — store a world as a *recipe* over canonical sources (git refs, schemas),
  never as an opaque snapshot.
- **I20** — scope state to the episode, and key each observation by the full history.
- **I39** — anti-Goodhart: prove a metric is discriminative with counterfactuals
  before you optimize against it.

## Where the pieces sit

Seen through this frame, the things already on disk line up:

| Asset | Today | In the meta frame |
|---|---|---|
| AgentMercury, 7 domains | worked examples of good worlds | planet's gold demonstrations |
| env-synth / dive-synth | the pipeline that builds s₀ and R | planet's runtime reference |
| priors/INVARIANTS.md (40) | how those domains actually got better | the rulebook for "how to build" |
| priors/checklist.py (8 gates) | auto-scores a world against the rules | R_meta |
| Track-C g_sft.jsonl (790 pairs) | intent → world-edit diffs | planet's SFT seed |

The 40 invariants aren't armchair rules. They're distilled from ~2,127 commits
across the five source repos, through 49 evidence-checked "arc notes" and 400
high-relevance change-unit lessons, annotated for \$8.54, with 1,362 of the
`repo@sha` citations verified to resolve. Track-C turns that same history into
supervised (intent → unified-diff) pairs — a change-unit is literally a
demonstration of the planet role: given a repo and a design intent, produce the edit.
(Honest caveat: those 790 pairs are multi-label, and only 224 are actually tagged
`planet`; they live in `history_mining`, not the MCP domain.)

## The trap, and what I actually know

The trap I keep reminding myself about: planet has to emit `s₀` and `R`
*coherently together*, not one and then the other — a gorgeous company database with
a rubric that doesn't bind to it is worthless. And the part worth teaching the model
is the non-trivial invariants; entity-sampling and field-filling the pipeline already
nails. The one test I have that points the right way is a re-derivation A/B: a fresh
agent, handed the priors, designed a world-generator for a brand-new domain
(warehouse fulfillment), and three blind judges scored it 20/20 on a 20-item pitfall
rubric versus 17.3 for the control. I don't want to oversell it — the control agent
also read the invariants file off the shared disk, so it's really "directed vs
incidental use," a delta of 2.7, not priors-vs-nothing.

And the honest status, because this is a notebook and not a pitch: the tri-role model
is a *thesis*, not a trained system. Of the three roles, only π is mature.

I tested **W**, the learned world model, end to end, and it came back a characterized
negative. Feed a frozen policy served-W observations instead of the real oracle's,
grade on the true end state, and W solved 0 of 40 tasks while the oracle solved 32;
reward-agreement sat at 0.20, which is just the oracle-fail floor, indistinguishable
from a noise-W. The one family that beat the structural null (group-by aggregation,
+8.3% at 4B) turned out to be prior-mode chance — W simply guesses count ≈ 0.70 ×
total, and the single task both the 4B and the 35B "carried" happens to have true
ratio 84/120 = exactly 0.700. So I don't get to conclude "bigger world models don't
help": the eval itself couldn't test capacity — 0 of 36 of the task's group-count
pairs appear anywhere in training. What I ship is the *signature*, not a verdict —
"heuristic-not-world-model": create-id predicted at 1.0 because it's a deterministic
rule, real dynamics at the floor. **G**, the world-maker, is severable and only
Stage-1 done (gate-pass 0.50 → 0.83); Stage-2 waits on a served Solar checkpoint.

What I'll check next. The first real test of the meta level: train the world-maker on
g_sft, reward it with the battery, and see whether it can reproduce a *held-out*
AgentMercury domain — can it rebuild the τ bank, or an office world, from intent
alone? And the worry underneath the whole recursion is whether R_meta survives being
optimized against. The moment planet learns to *game* the battery, the self-similar
loop starts rewarding broken worlds — Goodhart on the reward channel itself. That's
exactly why the next program (R9) is a controlled study of reward-channel integrity,
deterministic oracle versus LLM-judge, before I let anything close the loop on itself.
